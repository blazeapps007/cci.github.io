Cryptocurrency Fundamentals

This section provides the foundational knowledge required to understand cryptocurrency. We will cover what it is, the different types you may encounter, the technology that powers it, and the critical distinction between native assets (coins) and tokens.

What is Cryptocurrency?

A digital or virtual currency that uses cryptography for security. It operates independently of a central bank or government (decentralized).

  • ●
    Digital: Exists only in digital form.
  • ●
    Encrypted: Secured by complex cryptographic techniques.
  • ●
    Peer-to-Peer: Transactions occur directly between users.

Common Types

Illicit actors may use various types of cryptocurrencies for different purposes. Understanding them is key.

  • Payment Coins: For value transfer (e.g., Bitcoin, Litecoin).
  • Stablecoins: To avoid volatility by pegging to fiat (e.g., USDT, USDC). Often used in laundering.
  • Privacy Coins: To obscure transaction details (e.g., Monero, Zcash).
  • Meme Coins: Highly volatile, community-driven (e.g., Dogecoin).

Native Assets (Coins) vs. Tokens

This is a crucial distinction. A coin is native to its blockchain, while a token is built on top of an existing one. Click the cards to learn more.

Native Chain Asset (Coin)

(e.g., BTC, ETH)

The primary currency of a blockchain. Used to pay for network transaction fees ("gas") and to secure the network. Each blockchain has only one native coin.

Token

(e.g., USDT, SHIB)

Represents an asset or utility built on an existing blockchain (like Ethereum). Thousands of different tokens can exist on a single blockchain.

The Investigative Process

Tracing and prosecuting this type of crime requires a multi-faceted approach. This section outlines the key investigative techniques. Click on each tab to explore the different methods law enforcement can employ to dismantle TargetX's network.

Undercover P2P Trades

Law enforcement can pose as buyers or sellers in P2P markets. The goal is to directly interact with the "smurfs" to gather intelligence, identify their payment methods, and link their online personas to real-world banking information. This is a crucial first step in unmasking the lower-level operatives.

Leveraging Exchange Data

Submit official legal requests to exchanges like Binance, MEXC, and Poloniex for user data. This includes KYC (Know Your Customer) information, which can provide names, addresses, and official documents. Transaction histories from the exchange can help map the entire network of smurfs and identify the consolidation wallets.

Following the Fiat Trail

Collaborate with the Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) to analyze bank records of suspected smurfs. Investigators should look for patterns, such as many small, unrelated deposits followed by immediate transfers out to individuals identified through P2P trades. This links the digital smurfing activity to the traditional financial system.

On-Chain Forensics

Use specialized blockchain analysis tools (e.g., Chainalysis, Elliptic) to trace the flow of cryptocurrency. Starting from the smurfs' deposit addresses on exchanges, investigators can follow the funds as they move to consolidation wallets and ultimately to the final wallets controlled by TargetX. This provides immutable, court-admissible evidence of the money trail.

Legal Framework & Asset Recovery in Nepal

A successful prosecution depends on a strong understanding of Nepal's legal landscape regarding cryptocurrency and the procedures for asset recovery. All cryptocurrency activities are strictly prohibited, providing a clear basis for legal action.

Relevant Laws

  • Nepal Rastra Bank Act, 2002: Prohibits any currency transaction not approved by the central bank.
  • Foreign Exchange (Regulation) Act, 2019: Restricts foreign exchange, which includes crypto.
  • Act Restricting Investment Abroad, 1964: Prohibits foreign investment, including crypto purchases.

Severe Penalties:

Violators face heavy fines, imprisonment, and full confiscation of assets.

Asset Recovery Process

  1. Seizure: Once wallets are identified, law enforcement can legally seize the crypto assets. This may involve taking physical control of hardware wallets or compelling transfers.
  2. Forfeiture: Follow legal procedures to have the seized assets officially forfeited to the state.
  3. Repatriation: The recovered cryptocurrency can be liquidated, and the funds returned to the Government of Nepal's treasury.
  4. "Whitened" Money: If funds have been moved back to fiat and invested in assets like real estate, traditional financial investigation is required to trace and seize these properties.
